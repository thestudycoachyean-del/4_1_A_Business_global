What is Economic Growth?

Measuring national output and comparing global performance velocity

Key Definition

The growth rate of a country is measured by the annual percentage change in its Gross Domestic Product (GDP) — the total value of all goods and services produced within an economy over one year.

Key Definition

Emerging economies are countries characterized by rapid industrialisation and high economic growth rates, yet balancing relatively lower GDP per capita compared to mature nations.

🏭 Factors Driving Emerging Growth

  • Rapid expansion of secondary manufacturing powerhouses.
  • Inbound FDI looking to exploit structural cost advantages.
  • Favorable demographics alongside massive rural-to-urban shifts.

🇬🇧 Structural Realities of Mature Nations

  • Highly specialized tertiary (service-dominant) economic profiles.
  • Slowing expansion curves due to a highly mature institutional state.
  • Higher baseline production wages increasing domestic cost structures.
📋 Additional Spec Content — Common Features of Emerging Economies
  • Economic transformation: Transitioning rapidly from agricultural/primary production to manufacturing and service industries.
  • Rapid industrialisation: Heavy investment in infrastructure, factories, and urban development.
  • Growing middle class: Rising disposable incomes create vast new consumer markets for domestic and foreign brands.
  • Volatile exchange rates & institutions: Financial systems and legal frameworks that are developing but can experience policy shifts or inflation.

The 4 Key Indicators of Growth

How economists evaluate development beyond simple output figures

💵

GDP Per Capita

Total economic output divided by population. Measures average income per head.

📚

Literacy Rates

Percentage of the population who can read and write. Indicates workforce skill capability.

🏥

Health Indicators

Life expectancy and infant mortality. Reflects infrastructure quality and well-being.

🌟

HDI

Human Development Index. Combines income, life expectancy, and schooling into a score from 0 to 1.

Advantage Core Models

Distinguishing competitive strategies from macro endowment systems

Concept Type Comparative Advantage Competitive Advantage
Structural Focus Ability to generate specific outputs at a lower comparative opportunity cost baseline. Business-specific strategies (differentiation/cost leadership) driving asymmetric margins over sector rivals.
Strategic Driver Natural factor endowments (climate, specialized geography, resource depths). Operational execution, distinct intellectual properties, structural brand equity.

🏢 Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) Mechanisms

Inward Investment

External capital entering a domestic market to set up assets. Triggers job architecture creation and vertical technology dispersion transfers.

Outward Investment

Domestic firms deploying capital structures into external sovereign landscapes to acquire strategic resource footprints or set up offshore branches.

The Core Engines of Integration

The structural forces behind global market compression

🔓 Liberalisation

The systematic unwinding of tariffs and quotas via WTO negotiation tracks, transforming historically insulated zones into open networks.

📦 Containerisation

The standardization of shipping containers. This radically collapsed the cost of long-distance cargo handling and transport logistics.

💻 Tech & Comms

Real-time vertical tracking and internet backbones allow head offices to seamlessly manage complex, multi-tiered global supply chains.

💡 Examiner Application Strategy

Linking Globalisation to Operating Costs

When analyzing business strategy, do not just say "globalisation increases sales." Anchor your analysis chains in how containerisation and communication networks allow firms to fragment their value chains—sourcing materials where they are cheapest and keeping inventory levels highly lean.

The Protectionist Toolkit

How states systematically intervene in cross-border commerce

🧾

Tariffs

Customs duties or import taxes placed on incoming foreign goods to artificially push up their retail price.

🛑

Quotas

Strict quantitative ceilings restricting the absolute physical volume of a specific good allowed past borders.

💰

Subsidies

Direct financial assistance grants paid to domestic firms to lower their operating costs and make them more price-competitive.

📋

Barriers

Complex administrative rules, strict labeling conditions, or excessive safety certifications designed to stall import flows.

Stakeholder Group Negative Consequences Positive Outcomes
Domestic Consumers Face reduced choice and higher retail prices due to insulated, uncompetitive market environments. Can see temporary insulation of localized employment opportunities in protected sectors.
Foreign Exporters Face severe margin pressure or loss of market access as their price advantages are neutralized. Are incentivized to build production facilities directly inside the target country (tariff-jumping FDI).

Global Trading Blocs

Examining the structural integration formats of key regional partnerships

🇪🇺 European Union
  • A deep single market framework.
  • Full harmonization of technical standards.
  • Guarantees the free movement of goods, labor, services, and capital.
    • 🇺🇸 USMCA
  • Eliminates internal tariffs between the US, Mexico, and Canada.
  • Each member retains complete autonomy over its external tariff rates with outside countries.
    • 🌏 ASEAN
  • Accelerates trade integration across Southeast Asia.
  • Fosters massive manufacturing investment networks.

    • 📈 Commercial Advantages

    • Access to a much larger, tariff-free regional consumer pool.
    • Opportunities to achieve greater internal economies of scale.
    • Streamlined, low-friction supply chains across member countries.

    📉 Commercial Risks

    • Intense competition from lower-cost producers within the bloc.
    • Compliance costs tied to strict regional regulatory standards.
    • Trade Diversion: Being forced to buy from inefficient regional partners over cheaper global suppliers outside the bloc due to external tariffs.
